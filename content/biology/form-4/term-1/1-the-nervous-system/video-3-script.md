[SCENE 2 — CONCEPT BREAKDOWN: The Brain as an Organisation]

PRESENTER:
"Let me give you an analogy. Think of the brain like a company. A big organisation. Five departments. Each one has a job."

(pause)

"Department one — the cerebral hemispheres. This is the CEO's office. The cerebral hemispheres handle the big decisions: thinking, reasoning, memory, language, voluntary movement. Everything conscious happens here. The boss lives here."

[Visual note: label 'cerebral hemispheres' at the top of the brain diagram, with icons for thought and memory]

"Department two — the hypothalamus. Think of this as the internal systems manager. The hypothalamus regulates body temperature, hunger, thirst, and sleep. It monitors the body's internal environment and keeps everything in balance. Without this department, the whole company falls apart."

[Visual note: highlight hypothalamus, small structure near centre of brain]

"Department three — the pituitary gland. Now this one is attached to the hypothalamus. The pituitary gland is the hormone secretary. It controls and releases hormones — it tells other glands in the body what to do. This is actually where the nervous system and the endocrine system connect. We'll come back to that."

[Visual note: show pituitary gland just below hypothalamus, with arrows pointing outward to represent hormone release]

"Department four — the cerebellum. Down at the back. This is the movement coordinator. The cerebellum controls balance and coordination. Every time you walk, ride a bike, or — let's be honest — try to dance — the cerebellum is working. It makes sure your movements are smooth and coordinated."

[Visual note: highlight cerebellum at the rear-lower portion of the brain]

"And department five — the medulla oblongata. This is the life support team. The medulla oblongata controls the automatic functions you don't think about: breathing, heartbeat, blood pressure, swallowing. If this department goes down, everything stops. It's that serious."

[Visual note: highlight medulla oblongata at the base of the brain, connecting to spinal cord]

(pause)

"Now — the syllabus also groups these into two larger regions. The forebrain includes the cerebrum — which is the same as the cerebral hemispheres — plus the hypothalamus and the pituitary gland. The hindbrain includes the cerebellum and the medulla oblongata. Front of the brain, back of the brain. Simple split."

[Visual note: split diagram into forebrain (front, highlighted in one colour) and hindbrain (back, highlighted in another colour)]

[SCENE 3 — DRUG EFFECTS BREAKDOWN]

PRESENTER:
"Okay. Now drugs."

(pause)

"I'm not here to lecture you. You've heard it before. What I want to give you is the science — because the science is actually what the exam tests, and it's also genuinely interesting."

"The syllabus breaks drugs into three categories. Let's go through each one."

[Visual note: show a three-column table: Depressants | Stimulants | Hallucinogens]

"Category one — depressants. Examples: alcohol, heroin. Depressants slow down the nervous system. They increase reaction time — meaning your responses become slower. That's why driving under the influence of alcohol is dangerous. Your nervous system literally takes longer to respond to what your eyes are seeing. The brain is there. The signals are arriving. But the response is delayed."

(pause)

"Category two — stimulants. Examples: cocaine, caffeine, nicotine. These do the opposite. Stimulants increase motor activity and heart rate, and they can improve attention — at least temporarily. Your brain on caffeine is basically you during exam week: alert, focused, but also kind of shaky and slightly too intense. That's stimulants. They push the nervous system into overdrive."

[Visual note: show upward arrows next to 'heart rate', 'motor activity', 'attention' in the stimulants column]

"Category three — hallucinogens. Example from the syllabus: marijuana. Hallucinogens distort judgment. They interfere with how the brain processes information — specifically in the cerebral hemispheres — so your perception of reality gets distorted. Your decision-making becomes unreliable. This is why the effects can feel unpredictable."

(pause)

"And there is one more — tetanus. Not a drug, but the syllabus wants you to know it. Tetanus is a bacterial infection. The toxin produced by the bacteria damages brain cells and impairs nerve tissue. That impairment is what causes the muscle spasms associated with tetanus. The nervous system is being directly attacked."

[Visual note: show a simple warning icon alongside the word 'tetanus' with the phrase 'bacterial toxin → nerve tissue damage']

[SCENE 4 — EXAM-STYLE EXAMPLE]

PRESENTER:
"Let's do the exam question now. This is exactly the kind of thing you'll see."

[Visual note: show question on screen in a text box]

"Question: A student drinks alcohol before a race. Explain how this affects his nervous system and why his performance suffers."

(pause)

"Here's how you answer this — step by step."

"Step one: identify the drug category. Alcohol is a depressant."

"Step two: state the effect on the nervous system. Depressants increase reaction time. The nervous system's response to stimuli becomes slower."

"Step three: connect it to the specific scenario. In a race, the student needs fast reflexes and quick coordination — controlled by the cerebral hemispheres and cerebellum. With reaction time increased and coordination impaired, his performance will be significantly worse."

"That's a full-mark answer. Category — effect — application. Three steps."

[Visual note: show the three steps on screen as the presenter names them]

[SCENE 5 — CONNECTION TO EARLIER SECTIONS]

PRESENTER:
"Now — one last thing. The nervous system versus the endocrine system."

"In the earlier sections, you learned how the nervous system sends messages using electrical impulses along neurones — fast, precise, short-lived. The endocrine system does something similar, but using hormones released into the bloodstream — slower, broader, longer-lasting."

"Where do they connect? Right at the hypothalamus and pituitary gland. The hypothalamus receives nerve signals, then triggers the pituitary gland to release hormones. One message, two systems. That's the bridge."
