[SCENE 2 — EXAMPLE 1: KNEE JERK REFLEX]
PRESENTER:
"Example one. The knee jerk reflex. You've seen this — doctor taps your knee with a little hammer, and your leg kicks forward before you even decide to move it. Why?"

(pause)

"Let's trace the path of the impulse. This is called the spinal reflex arc, and this is the exact sequence you need to know."

[Visual note: Animated diagram of the spinal reflex arc appears, lighting up each component as it's named]

"Step one. The hammer hits your knee. That's the stimulus — a physical tap on the patellar tendon."

(pause)

"Step two. A receptor in the tendon detects the stretch. Receptors pick up the stimulus. The receptor here is a stretch receptor in the muscle."

(pause)

"Step three. The receptor sends an impulse along the sensory neurone. The sensory neurone carries the signal toward the CNS — the central nervous system. In a spinal reflex, it travels to the spinal cord."

(pause)

"Step four. Inside the spinal cord, the impulse passes through a relay neurone. The relay neurone is the connector — it sits inside the CNS and links the sensory neurone to the motor neurone."

(pause)

"Step five. The relay neurone passes the impulse to the motor neurone. The motor neurone carries the signal away from the CNS, out to the effector."

(pause)

"Step six. The effector — in this case, the muscle in your thigh — receives the signal and contracts. Your leg kicks."

[Visual note: Full diagram now glowing — receptor, sensory neurone, relay neurone in spinal cord, motor neurone, effector — with arrows showing impulse direction]

"Receptor. Sensory neurone. CNS — spinal cord. Relay neurone. Motor neurone. Effector. That's the complete spinal reflex arc. And notice — your brain didn't make this decision. The signal went to the spinal cord and came straight back out. That's what makes it a spinal reflex."

---

[SCENE 3 — COMMON MISTAKE]
PRESENTER:
"Here's where most people mess up."

[Visual note: Red X appears over diagram showing impulse going to brain first]

"Students write that the impulse travels to the brain first, and then the brain sends a signal back. Wrong. In a spinal reflex, the impulse only goes to the spinal cord. The brain gets informed — but it's not in charge here. The response already happened before your brain even registered the tap."

(pause)

"Think of it like this — your friend texts you 'watch out!' but by the time you read the message, you already ducked. Your spinal cord ducked. Your brain just got the notification."

[Visual note: Phone notification icon appears briefly — then back to the reflex arc diagram]

"So when the exam asks where the impulse goes in a spinal reflex: spinal cord. Not brain."

---

[SCENE 4 — EXAMPLE 2: PUPIL REFLEX]
PRESENTER:
"Example two. The pupil reflex. This one's a cranial reflex — meaning the impulse travels to the brain, not the spinal cord. Different pathway, same principle."

[Visual note: Diagram showing eyes and the path to the brain — optic nerve highlighted]

"Scenario. You walk out of a dark room into bright sunlight. Your pupils shrink almost instantly. Here's the path."

(pause)

"Step one. The stimulus is bright light hitting your eye."

(pause)

"Step two. The receptor — photoreceptors in the retina of the eye — detect the light."

(pause)

"Step three. The impulse travels along the sensory neurone — specifically, the optic nerve — to the brain. This is a cranial reflex, so the CNS here is the brain, not the spinal cord."

(pause)

"Step four. The brain processes the signal and sends an impulse back along the motor neurone."

(pause)

"Step five. The effector — the circular muscles of the iris — contracts. The pupil gets smaller. Less light enters. Your eye is protected."

[Visual note: Diagram shows the full path — retina, optic nerve, brain, motor neurone to iris, pupil constricting]

"Same structure as the knee jerk — receptor, sensory neurone, CNS, motor neurone, effector. But this time the CNS is the brain, making it a cranial reflex, not a spinal reflex."

(pause)

"The path is the same. The location of the CNS involvement is what changes."
